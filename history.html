Discover the rich and fascinating past that shaped modern Macau.

Before Portuguese arrival, Macau was part of Xiangshan County and inhabited by fishermen and farmers. It was a quiet village with spiritual roots reflected in local temples like A-Ma.

In the 16th century, Portuguese traders established Macau as a vital trading post between Europe and Asia, making it the first and last European colony in China.

The 1887 Treaty of Peking formally recognized Portugal‚Äôs perpetual occupation and administration of Macau, further solidifying its colonial status.

During World War II, Macau remained neutral and became a sanctuary for refugees fleeing Japanese-occupied territories in mainland China and nearby regions.

In 1987, China and Portugal signed a Joint Declaration agreeing to the handover of Macau under the ‚ÄúOne Country, Two Systems‚Äù policy, ensuring a high degree of autonomy.

Throughout the centuries, Macau was a melting pot of cultures, where East met West not only in commerce but also in religion, cuisine, and everyday life. Temples like A-Ma symbolize the city's rich spiritual history.

In 1999, Macau was handed back to China, becoming a Special Administrative Region with its own legal and economic systems, preserving its unique historical identity and legacy.

The historic center of Macau showcases beautiful Portuguese architecture blended with Chinese elements, earning UNESCO World Heritage status in 2005.
